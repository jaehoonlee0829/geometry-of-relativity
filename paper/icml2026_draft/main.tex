\begin{abstract}
Large language models make context-sensitive scalar judgments: the same value can be described differently depending on the comparison class in the prompt.
This paper studies whether that behavior is reflected in internal representations for gradable adjectives such as \emph{tall}, \emph{rich}, and \emph{fast}.
Our current working hypothesis is that Gemma 2 represents relative standing using a context-normalized variable related to \zscore, while also relying on lexical and output-facing components.
The planned evidence combines behavioral grids, representation geometry, layerwise readouts, steering, lexical/residual decomposition, and sparse-feature analysis.
The key claim is intentionally provisional until the remaining controls and literature review decide which result is most novel.
\end{abstract}

\section{Background and Problem Setup}
\label{sec:setup}

\subsection{Gradable Adjectives as Context-Relative Judgments}
\label{subsec:gradable-adjectives}

Gradable adjectives depend on a comparison class.
For this paper, the operational question is whether the model's judgment depends on raw target value $x$, context mean $\mu$, context spread $\sigma$, or a normalized quantity such as \zscore.
% TODO(cite): Add gradable adjective / pragmatic thresholding literature after related-work review.

\subsection{Operational Variables}
\label{subsec:variables}

For each prompt, $x$ is the target value, $\mu$ is the context mean, $\sigma$ is the context spread, and $z$ is the experimenter's context-normalized standing:
\[
z = \frac{x-\mu}{\sigma}.
\]
The main behavioral output is the logit difference
\[
\logitdiff = \mathrm{logit}(\text{high adjective}) -
\mathrm{logit}(\text{low adjective}).
\]

\subsection{Evidence Standards}
\label{subsec:evidence-standards}

This skeleton separates four levels of evidence.
Behavioral evidence shows that outputs track $z$.
Readout evidence shows that $z$ is linearly available in activations.
Geometric evidence characterizes how the variable is organized across concepts and layers.
Causal evidence tests whether moving in candidate directions changes model behavior.
